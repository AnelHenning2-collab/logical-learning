What are the 4 control categories?

Control Categories organize security measures by their domain of application:

  • Technology — Hardware, software, firmware controls (firewalls, encryption, AV)
  • Management — Policies, procedures, risk assessment frameworks
  • Operational — Day-to-day processes run by people (monitoring, training, patching)
  • Physical — Barriers, locks, cameras, guards, environmental controls
What are the 6 control types?

Control Types describe the function or intent of a control:

  • Preventive — Stops the threat before it happens (e.g., firewall rules)
  • Deterrent — Discourages the threat actor (e.g., security signage)
  • Detective — Identifies that a threat is occurring or has occurred (e.g., IDS, logs)
  • Corrective — Fixes the issue after detection (e.g., patching, restoring backup)
  • Compensating — Alternative control when primary isn't feasible (e.g., extra monitoring when MFA unavailable)
  • Directive — Mandates specific behavior or actions (e.g., policies, acceptable-use agreements)
What is the CIA Triad?

The CIA Triad is the foundation of information security:

  • Confidentiality — Only authorized persons can access the data. Enforced via encryption, access controls, classification.
  • Integrity — Data hasn't been tampered with or altered. Enforced via hashing, checksums, digital signatures.
  • Availability — Systems and data are accessible when needed. Enforced via redundancy, backups, failover.

Authentication verifies identity ("Who are you?"), while Authorization determines access level ("What can you do?"). Both support the triad.

What is non-repudiation?

Non-repudiation ensures that an action taken cannot be denied or reversed by the party who performed it.

  • Example: A digital signature proves the sender authored a message — they can't later deny it
  • Implemented via digital signatures, audit logs, timestamps
  • Key point: Non-repudiation is separate from the CIA Triad — it's an additional security goal
What is a gap analysis?

Gap Analysis assesses the difference between the current security posture and the desired state.

  • Used after security incidents to understand why they happened despite controls in place
  • Identifies missing or weak controls, misconfigurations, and process failures
  • Drives remediation priorities and resource allocation
  • Answers: "We took these actions — what went wrong, and what's still missing?"
What cryptographic solutions protect data?

Cryptographic Solutions for data protection beyond standard encryption:

  • Obfuscation — Making data or code difficult to understand (code obfuscation, steganography)
  • Tokenization — Replacing sensitive data with non-sensitive placeholder tokens. The token maps back to the original via a secure vault. Common in payment processing (PCI DSS).
  • Data Masking — Hiding portions of data (e.g., ****-****-****-1234). The original data may still exist in the database but is not displayed.
Summary — Domain 1
Domain 1 establishes the security vocabulary: controls are organized by category (technology, management, operational, physical) and by type (prevent, deter, detect, correct, compensate, direct). The CIA Triad — Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability — underpins every security decision. Non-repudiation adds accountability beyond the triad. Gap analysis connects incidents to missing controls, and cryptographic solutions (obfuscation, tokenization, masking) protect data at multiple layers.

What are the 3 states of data?

Data exists in three states, each requiring different protection strategies:

  • Data at Rest — Stored on disk, database, USB, cloud storage. Protected via full-disk encryption (FDE), database encryption, access controls.
  • Data in Transit — Moving across a network (email, web traffic, file transfer). Protected via TLS/SSL, VPN tunnels, IPsec.
  • Data in Use — Actively being processed in memory/CPU. Hardest to protect. Solutions include secure enclaves, trusted execution environments (TEE), memory encryption.
What methods secure data across all states?

Methods to Secure Data — applied depending on state, sensitivity, and regulation:

  • Geographic considerations — Data sovereignty laws (GDPR requires EU data in EU). Location of data centers matters.
  • Encryption — Converts plaintext to ciphertext. AES-256 for at rest, TLS 1.3 for in transit.
  • Hashing — One-way function producing a fixed-length digest. Verifies integrity (SHA-256, SHA-3). Not reversible.
  • Masking — Shows partial data to authorized users. Original data preserved but hidden from display.
  • Tokenization — Swaps real data for a token. Vault stores mapping. Ideal for PCI compliance.
  • Obfuscation — Makes data/code unintelligible without proper key or context.
  • Segmentation — Network/data segmentation limits blast radius. VLANs, microsegmentation, subnets.
  • Permissions & Restrictions — RBAC, ACLs, least privilege, need-to-know. Fundamental access control layer.
Summary — Domain 3
Security Architecture focuses on protecting data across its three states: at rest, in transit, and in use. Each state demands different tools — from full-disk encryption and TLS to secure enclaves. The eight core methods (encryption, hashing, masking, tokenization, obfuscation, segmentation, permissions, and geographic compliance) form a layered defense. The exam tests your ability to match the right method to the right data state and regulatory requirement.

What are the 5 breach impact categories?

Breach Impact Categories — how organizations assess damage:

  • Financial — Loss of data value, system rebuild costs, contract & forensic expenses, incident response costs. Direct monetary impact.
  • Reputational — Negative press, loss of goodwill, eroded trust from customers, employers, suppliers, and stakeholders. Can trigger identity theft liabilities.
  • Strategic — Loss of operational efficiency, inability to execute the business plan, loss of strategic objectives and competitive advantage.
  • Operational — Day-to-day disruptions: delays, process breakdowns, inefficiencies, inability to deliver services.
  • Compliance — HIPAA violations, regulatory failures, legal consequences, fines, lawsuits, mandatory disclosures.
How do risk categories interconnect?

Risk Interconnection — risks don't stay in neat boxes. They cascade across categories:

  • A compliance failure (e.g., HIPAA breach) can trigger all other categories simultaneously
  • Financial penalties → strategic inability → operational delays → reputational damage
  • Reputational damage can cause customer churn → financial loss → strategic setback
  • The exam expects you to recognize that a single incident can have multi-category impact

Key takeaway: When assessing risk, don't stop at one impact category. Trace the chain reaction.

What financial costs follow a breach?

Financial impact of a breach includes both direct and indirect costs:

  • Data loss costs — Value of lost intellectual property, customer data, trade secrets
  • Rebuild costs — Restoring systems, reimaging devices, redeploying infrastructure
  • Contract costs — SLA penalties, breach of contract with vendors or clients
  • Forensic costs — Hiring incident response teams, digital forensics investigation
  • Incident response costs — Staff overtime, external consultants, communication efforts
  • Regulatory fines — GDPR up to 4% of annual global revenue, HIPAA up to $1.5M per violation category
How does a breach affect reputation?

Reputational impact is often the longest-lasting damage:

  • Negative PR — Media coverage amplifies the breach, sometimes disproportionately
  • Loss of goodwill — Years of brand trust destroyed in a single incident
  • Stakeholder trust erosion — Customers, employees, employers, suppliers, investors all lose confidence
  • Identity theft liability — If PII is exposed, the org may be liable for credit monitoring, lawsuits
  • Talent impact — Difficulty recruiting top security talent after a public breach
Summary — Domain 5
Security Program Management (20% of the exam) is about understanding risk holistically. The DAD Triangle (Disclose → Confidentiality, Alter → Integrity, Denial → Availability) maps attacks to CIA pillars. Breach impacts span five interconnected categories: financial, reputational, strategic, operational, and compliance. The critical insight is risk interconnection — a single compliance failure can cascade into all other categories. The exam tests your ability to trace these chain reactions and assess total organizational impact.
